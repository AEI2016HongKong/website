Our Story

We are a team of four students from Eckerd College who took part in the Asia & the Environment Initiative during the spring and summer of 2016. The initiative is split into two parts: a course held during the spring semester in St. Petersburg, Florida, wherein we studied the historical, socioeconomical, and environmental background of our host country and conducted research on contemporary environmental challenges of our choosing, and a month-long internship abroad where we performed labwork and fieldwork alongside students and professors from Hong Kong Baptist University in Kowloon Tong, Hong Kong. The research projects we worked on all focused on assessing and protecting Hong Kong's biodiversity, and in the process of doing so they took us to the farthest parts of the region. This website is dedicated to hosting our findings and our memories from the past six months.

Bioinformatics

by Clifford Lezark

My research focused on the recently-sequenced genomes of a few members of the freshwater-snail genus Pomacea. These snails are invasive species in Hong Kong, but a lack of proper research on the genus as a whole has made it difficult to counteract their spread throughout the region.

Under the direction of Dr. Jian-Wen Qiu, I was tasked with creating a publically-available database capable of storing the gene sequences of every Pomacea species they had identified, along with the annotations for those genes from databases around the world. The goal of the project was to provide enough data to catalyze new research on ways to drive out the invasive snails.

Landfill Assessment

by Miguel Ramos-Conde

Research at Hong Kong Baptist University focused on the impact of landfill usage within the soil. Aspects such as acidic level, organic life, and species diversity are taken into account when analyzing soil samples (from Country Park and landfill). Field work was conducted on the SENT (South East New Territories) landfill. Research was conducted in HKBU laboratory. The goal of the research is to take to discover if soil from the landfill is similar to the soil found in country parks (where no waste is being disposed).

Endangered Species

by Thompson Wright and Amelia Itteilag

The research we did in Hong Kong consisted of studying the behaviors and diets of different species of small local, endemic, and foreign animals including land snails, turtles, tree frogs, bull frogs, and lizards. The focus of Doctor Hei, the professor that we worked with at Hong Kong Baptist University, was mainly on local turtles and their illegal trade.

Doctor Hei worked to learn a great deal about illegal turtle trade locally, even heading to the markets to acquire information, and we helped him work to compile data on illegal turtle trade online using a reptile sale website. For our first project on land snails we went on a few trips to local natural parks, collected as many snails as we could find in a particular blocked out area. Later at the lab we searched through collected leaf litter to double check for any leftover snail shells.

We also collected information on Chinese Water Dragons and Chinese Bullfrogs, two foreign animals, to find out the effect they have on local and endemic species of frogs. We collected data on the diets of Chinese Water Dragons by catching them on night hikes and pumping their stomachs with water. Similarly, we took hikes to local parks and collected Chinese Bullfrogs in plastic bags, took their measurements later at the lab, and dissected them for the contents of their stomachs.
